Filing Your Taxes: A Beginner's Guide

Taxes can be complicated, but understanding the basics can save you time and money. This section covers key concepts for individual filers.

üë• Choosing Your Filing Status

Your filing status determines your standard deduction and tax rate. Choose the one that accurately reflects your marital and family situation on the last day of the tax year (December 31).

  • Single: Unmarried, divorced, or legally separated.
  • Married Filing Jointly (MFJ): Married couples who choose to file one combined return.
  • Married Filing Separately (MFS): Married couples who file two separate returns. This usually results in higher taxes but may be necessary in certain situations.
  • Head of Household (HOH): Unmarried and paying more than half the cost of keeping up a home for yourself and a qualifying person (e.g., a dependent child).
  • Qualifying Widow(er): If your spouse died, you can sometimes use MFJ rates for two years following the death, provided you meet certain requirements.

üí∞ Standard Deduction vs. Itemized Deductions

You can reduce your taxable income by claiming either the standard deduction or itemizing your deductions. You should choose the method that gives you the largest deduction.

Standard Deduction

A fixed dollar amount based on your filing status. This is the simplest option and is used by most taxpayers.

Itemized Deductions (Schedule A)

Requires tallying specific expenses, such as state and local taxes (SALT), mortgage interest, and charitable contributions. Only beneficial if your total itemized expenses exceed the standard deduction amount.
